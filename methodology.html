Project Methodology

Understanding our research process, data decisions, and analytical framework

Research Questions

This project is guided by three central questions:

  • How did textile handicraft traditions vary across ethnolinguistic groups and geographic regions in Nigeria?
  • How did British colonialism and foreign imports specifically disrupt traditional knowledge systems and women's labor practices?
  • Can digital mapping effectively visualize colonial economic disruption as a process rather than a singular event?

These questions drive our data collection, geospatial analysis, and interpretive framework, centering women's expertise and labor within broader colonial histories.

Sources & Data

Our data is drawn from multiple archival and scholarly sources:

Archival Materials
  • British Colonial Office papers (National Archives, UK)
  • Missionary records and photographs (Rhodes House Library, Oxford)
  • Trade reports and economic surveys
Museum Collections
  • British Museum Nigerian textile collections
  • Smithsonian National Museum of African Art
  • Field Museum, Chicago
Scholarly Sources
  • Published ethnographic studies
  • Contemporary historical analyses
  • Oral histories collected by Nigerian scholars

Critical Approach: We read colonial archives "against the grain," centering women's expertise despite archival bias and seeking voices that colonial documentation marginalized or dismissed.

Analytical Approach

Our methodology combines spatial humanities, feminist historiography, and postcolonial analysis:

Geospatial Mapping

We use Leaflet.js and GeoJSON to map textile traditions across geographic regions, connecting material culture to specific communities and ethnolinguistic groups. Historical maps are georeferenced using QGIS to provide temporal context.

Temporal Analysis

The project visualizes change across five time periods (Pre-1850, 1850-1885, 1885-1920, 1920-1960, Post-1960), showing how colonial policies transformed production patterns over time rather than presenting colonialism as a singular disruptive event.

Centering Women's Labor

We foreground women's expertise, economic contributions, and knowledge systems that colonial archives often marginalized. Each location entry documents both the craft tradition and its practitioners.

Limitations

We acknowledge several limitations in our methodology and data:

Archival Gaps
  • Colonial archives privilege European perspectives and male actors
  • Many regions and time periods lack comprehensive documentation
  • Women's voices are often mediated through colonial observers
Georeferencing Challenges
  • Historical place names don't always correspond to precise modern coordinates
  • Ethnic territories were fluid and contested, not fixed boundaries
  • Some craft traditions were practiced across wide geographic areas
Temporal Uncertainty
  • Not all sources provide exact dates for craft practices
  • Change occurred at different rates in different regions
  • Some traditions persisted or revived in ways difficult to document
Transparency: Where data is incomplete or uncertain, we document these gaps explicitly rather than making unsubstantiated claims. Our goal is to present the most accurate picture possible while acknowledging the constraints of the historical record.
